The challenge

Selling eyewear online when no one thought it could work

When Warby Parker launched, less than 2.5% of glasses were sold online. The company's foundational bet was that customers could be trusted to try on frames at home — but the online experience had to earn that trust at every step. The challenge was designing a digital service that bridged physical and digital: helping customers confidently select frames, understand the process, complete a return, and convert to purchase, without ever touching a product first.

This was not a UI problem. It was a service design problem — one that required coordinating digital flows, physical packaging, email communications, logistics operations, and mobile app design into a single coherent experience.

Customer journey

Seven stages, one continuous thread

The HTO experience spanned days, not minutes. Customers moved between screens, physical objects, and conversations with friends — and each transition was a moment where the service could earn or lose trust.

Outcomes

What the design work produced

The online HTO experience was foundational to Warby Parker's DTC model for 14 years. Its eventual sunset — as 300+ physical stores made the program less necessary — was itself a product of its success.

01

Reduced selection anxiety

Surfacing the style quiz and virtual try-on early in the funnel helped customers confidently narrow to five frames before committing — improving box quality and downstream conversion rates.

02

Streamlined return-to-purchase path

Customers returning their box could complete a purchase in a single flow using saved frame preferences — reducing the drop-off between physical try-on and checkout.

03

Built trust through transparency

Clear timelines, prepaid return labels, and proactive communications addressed the primary anxiety of buying eyewear without trying it in person — removing the reasons not to proceed.

04

Supported program scale for 14 years

The online HTO became foundational to Warby Parker's DTC model, serving 2.6M+ active customers and underpinning the company's growth at a time when online eyewear represented less than 2.5% of the market.

05

Informed strategic evolution

Usage data from the HTO experience helped Warby Parker make a confident strategic decision to invest in physical retail expansion and AR virtual try-on — and eventually sunset the program when 300+ stores covered most of the geographic need.

Transferable principles

What payments design and HTO design have in common

Consumer payments and Home Try-On are structurally the same design problem: a user must hand over something of value — money, personal data, time — in exchange for an outcome they can't fully verify in advance. Trust is the product. Every design decision either earns it or erodes it.

Warby Parker · Home Try-On

Designing for deferred commitment

Customers select five frames without buying — deferring financial commitment while building confidence. Every touchpoint had to sustain intent across a multi-day gap.

Google Payments · Buyflows

Designing for zero-friction commitment

A payment flow must compress decision and action into seconds — Autofill, tap-to-pay, one-tap checkout. The design challenge is sustaining trust while removing all hesitation points.

Both require earning trust at the moment of highest stakes
Warby Parker · Home Try-On

Invisible logistics, seamless experience

Carrier APIs, return processing, Rx verification, and inventory management all had to work invisibly. When backstage broke, the customer experience broke — even if the UI was fine.

Google Payments · Platform

Invisible infrastructure, secure experience

Tokenisation, fraud detection, bank authorization, and money movement happen below the line of visibility. The designer's job is to ensure none of that complexity surfaces as user anxiety.

Service blueprinting surfaces what breaks the experience before users find it
Warby Parker · Home Try-On

The return confirmation as peak-intent moment

Returning a box was the highest-intent moment in the journey — customers had just held the frames. Designing that confirmation as a conversion trigger, not just a receipt, was the critical insight.

Google Payments · Buyflows

The payment confirmation as relationship moment

A payment confirmation is the highest-trust moment in a transaction — the user just committed. What happens in the three seconds after "Payment sent" shapes whether they trust the platform next time.

Peak-moment design determines whether users return
